Women in Biology


Jane Goodall

(1934-Present)

Primatologist & Anthropologist.

Jane Goodall is known world-wide for her groundbreaking studies on primates. She is considered the top expert on chimpanzeees in the world and is perhaps best known for her 45 year study on the social lives of these animals in Tanzania.

Marie Maynard Daly

(1921-2003)

Biochemist

Marie Maynard Daly was the first Black American woman in the United States to earn a Ph.D. in chemistry (awarded by Columbia University in 1947).She studied histones, proteins found in cell nuclei, and was able to show the amino acid composition of various histone fractions. She suggested that histones were a mixture of basic components such as lysine and argenine. Histones have since been shown to be important in gene expression. Daly's work on histones is now considered fundamental.

Rita Levi-Montalcini

(1909-2012)

Neurobiologist

Rita Levi-Montalcini was a neurologist who won the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine in 1986 for her findings in nerve growth factor (NGF). She was the first Nobel laureate to live past her 100th birthday.

Flossie-Wong Staal

(1947-present)

Virologist and Molecular Biologist

In 1983, Wong-Staal and her team of scientists identified HIV as the cause of AIDS, simultaneously with French virologist Luc Montagnier. Wong-Staal would later become the first scientist to clone HIV, and developed a complete genetic map of the virus. Her work made it possible for HIV tests to be developed and implemented throughout the world.

Barbara McClintock

(1902-1992)

Cytogeneticist

McClintock's was awarded the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine in 1983 for the discovery of genetic transposition; the only woman to receive an unshared Nobel Prize in that category.

Elizabeth Blackburn

(1948-Present)

Molecular Biologist

Elizabeth Blackburn won the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine in 2009 for her discovery of telomerase which is the enzyme which replenishes telomere. Telomere is part of the end of a chromosome which protects them.
